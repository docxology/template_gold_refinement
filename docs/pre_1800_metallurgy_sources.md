# Pre-1800 Metallurgy Sources

This source review records how pre-1800 gold-refinement research may support
the manuscript analogy without expanding the local software-certification
claim.

| Source | Date | Safe support | Boundary |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Pliny the Elder, *Natural History*, Book XXXIII | c. 77 CE | Ancient source-domain vocabulary for metals, gold extraction, and touchstone testing | Descriptive natural history, not a procedural assay manual |
| Vannoccio Biringuccio, *De la Pirotechnia* | 1540 | Printed Renaissance metallurgy; mining, metalwork, smelting, and salt-based separation context | Italian metallurgical treatise, not a universal process standard |
| Georgius Agricola, *De re metallica* | 1556 | Staged mining, assaying, smelting, cupellation, and parting operations | Early modern technical sequence, not modern chemical theory |
| W. B. / William Badcock, *A Touch-Stone for Gold and Silver Wares* | 1678 | Goldsmith standards, fineness, weights, statutes, and counterfeit-coin checks | English goldsmithing and legal context only |
| Johann Andreas Cramer, *Elements of the Art of Assaying Metals* | 1741 | Eighteenth-century assay as a theory/practice discipline | Assay discipline, not evidence that all assayers used one standard method |
| Goldsmiths' Company Assay Office, "History of Hallmarking" | modern chronology of pre-1800 milestones | Institutional marking: 1238 regulation, 1300 leopard's-head mark, 1478 assay-office consolidation, 1576/1697/1798 standards | London hallmarking history, not a universal hallmarking regime |

The manuscript can use these sources to claim that staged refinement, testing,
fineness standards, and public marking are historically grounded. It must not
use them to claim that the exemplar's five stages are a literal historical
production recipe, that pre-1800 writers had modern chemical theories, or that
a rendered manuscript receives any legal, guild, market, or mint authority.
